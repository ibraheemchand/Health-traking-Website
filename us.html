BMI Introduction

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value derived from an adult's height and weight, used by healthcare professionals as a screening tool to estimate body fat and categorize weight status. It is calculated as weight in kilograms divided by the square of height in meters. A BMI under 18.5 is underweight, 18. to 24.9 range is normal, 25 to 29.9 range is overweight, and 30+ is obese

Understanding BMI Results

Risks Associated with Being Overweight

Being overweight increases the risk of serious diseases and health conditions, including:

  • High blood pressure and high "bad" (LDL) cholesterol
  • Type II diabetes and Coronary heart disease
  • Stroke and Gallbladder disease
  • Sleep apnea and breathing problems
  • Mental illnesses such as clinical depression and anxiety
  • Increased risk of mortality compared to those with a healthy BMI

Risks Associated with Being Underweight

Being underweight also carries significant health risks:

  • Malnutrition, vitamin deficiencies, and anemia
  • Osteoporosis (bone weakness)
  • Decreased immune function
  • Growth and development issues in children and teenagers
  • Increased risk of mortality compared to those with a healthy BMI

Limitations of BMI

BMI is a useful indicator but has limitations because it measures excess weight rather than excess body fat:

  • Body Composition: It does not distinguish between muscle, bone mass, and fat.
  • Athletes: Muscular individuals may have a high BMI but low body fat.
  • Age and Sex: Older adults and women tend to have more body fat than others with the same BMI.
